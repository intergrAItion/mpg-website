## Key Questions to Ask Before Signing Up

Before committing to any property management company, ask a few direct questions. Find out exactly what is included in the management fee and what will be billed additionally. Ask how maintenance requests are handled, including response times and how contractors are selected. Ask what your monthly statements will look like and how often you will receive updates about your property.

Also ask about their experience with your type of property. A company that primarily manages small residential units may not be the right fit for a block of flats or a commercial building. Understanding their background upfront will save you from discovering the mismatch after you have already signed.

## Red Flags That Signal a Poor Manager

Vague contracts are one of the clearest warning signs. If the management agreement does not clearly specify what is included, how fees are calculated, and how disputes are handled, that ambiguity will work against you.

Poor communication before you even sign up is another red flag. If responses are slow during the sales process, they are unlikely to improve once you are a client. A good property manager should be easy to reach and responsive from the first interaction.

Watch out for agents who are slow to address tenant complaints or maintenance issues. Unresolved problems escalate quickly, creating legal risk and damaging your property's condition. Ask potential managers how they handle common tenant disputes and what their typical resolution timeframe looks like.

## What Good Communication Should Look Like

You should never have to chase your property manager for basic information. A well-run agency will send monthly statements without being prompted, flag issues before they become emergencies, and keep you informed about any changes to your tenancy.

Good communication also means being reachable. You should be able to contact your property manager by phone, WhatsApp, or email and receive a response within a few hours during business hours. If that is not happening, your investment is not being managed properly.

Regular inspection reports, with photos and written summaries, should be provided after every scheduled inspection. These protect both you and your tenants by creating a clear record of the property's condition over time.

## How to Evaluate Value Beyond the Fee

The cheapest management fee is not always the best deal. An agent charging 8 percent who collects rent on time, keeps tenants happy, and prevents costly maintenance issues will deliver far more value than an agent charging 10 percent who misses arrears and lets small problems become large repairs.

When evaluating value, consider how proactive the agent is, how quickly they fill vacancies, and how long tenants tend to stay. Long tenancies reduce your turnover costs significantly, and a good property manager plays a direct role in achieving them.
